Practice Tips

Learning how to develop your ears may seem a difficult task. You might even be worried that you’re tone deaf. But once you understand what ear training actually involves, you will realize that you have already been training your ears your entire life! Actively practicing ear training is just a more effective way to do it.

Whether your dream is to play in an orchestra, a jazz ensemble, a garage band, or just write songs for your own pleasure, your musicality will benefit from ear training.

Here are some general tips to help you on your ear training journey, as well as some tips to integrate your training into your music practice.

Set Goals

One of the least effective things you can do is go into your practice room and just sit down and start playing. In order to make the most of your time, you need to make a plan as to what it is that you need to and want to accomplish in that time.

Do you want to memorise a piece? Do you want to broaden your jazz language by transcribing some of your favourite solos? If you go into the practice room with a goal in mind, you will be focused and motivated to attain that goal.

Reference songs

Using interval reference songs is a great way to get started in interval ear training, but often the examples given aren’t songs you know or particularly like. Work out the intervals used at the start of songs you do like, and assemble your own personal set of interval recognition songs to use. Interval training will be a lot more fun once you have!

Sing what you play

If you play Piano or Guitar (or generally any instrument that doesn’t require your breath!) practice singing the melodies that you’re playing as you play them. This encourages active listening, and greatly helps to strengthen the connection between your brain and your fingers.

With this type of practice, you’ll find that you’ll be able to remember and recite the melodies you’re practicing much more efficiently.

Go Slow

When you’re in your practice session, it’s tempting to get excited and dive straight into learning and reciting your music at lightning speed, because who doesn’t like a challenge? However this is counter-productive. Imagine practice is like sitting on a train.

When the train is going full speed and you look out your window from your seat, all you see is a blur. But when the train begins to slow down, you begin to see the details in the world around you. Effective practice should be like being on that slow train. Only by practicing slowly can you absorb the details of your subject.

Be Inspired

Inspiration is as important as practice when it comes to unlocking your creativity. Finding artists who really speak to you is vital to nourishing the curiosity and passion for music and your instrument, and it’s important for a good musician to have a broad repertoire of tunes in their arsenal.

Taking the time to discover and listen to great musicians can be the key to discovering yourself! Plus, it feels really good when you find that tune which really sings to you.
